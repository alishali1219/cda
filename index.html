MTO Approved Driving School

Why Choose Us?

The uXperience Design-a-thon, hosted by UW Stratford and SunLife, challenged teams to address financial stress among millennials. So, my team and I designed Island Funds, a gamified app that helps users build healthy money habits through collaborative goal-setting and personalized financial literacy.

The Problem

For many millennials, managing money feels overwhelming. Financial advice is often confusing, resources are scattered, and there's little motivation to stay on track. Despite growing stress around debt and saving, there’s no accessible platform that makes financial literacy engaging, social, and easy to stick with.

The Solution

Introducing Island Funds, the app we designed to help millennials manage financial stress and build financial literacy through gamified, collaborative experiences that make learning about money engaging and approachable.

Reflection

Upon reflection, I realized that our initial design for Island Funds focused heavily on the ideal user journey, where users are motivated to set goals, track progress, and explore financial literacy resources regularly, and overlooked some real-world challenges that could impact adoption and sustained engagement. Here are a few areas that I believe need further development:

  • Adoption and motivation: While gamification and social features encourage participation, attracting an initial user base may require partnerships with schools, employers, or financial institutions. Early-stage campaigns, incentives, or integrations with existing platforms could help drive engagement.
  • Personalization depth: The current onboarding process tailors content to a user’s stated needs, but financial situations can change quickly. Adding adaptive recommendations that respond to life events, like job changes, unexpected expenses, or new savings goals, would make the app more relevant over time.
  • Collaboration features: Group goals encourage social accountability, but they also introduce challenges such as varying commitment levels and coordination across schedules. Tools for role assignments, reminders, and progress visibility would make teamwork smoother and more effective.
  • Education and accessibility: Some users may lack basic financial knowledge, while others may already be advanced. Including tiered learning resources, beginner-friendly explanations, and interactive modules could make the platform more inclusive and useful for a wider audience.

This project helped me understand that creating a gamified financial tool is not just about good UI and engaging interactions. It is also about designing systems that build trust, adapt to changing needs, and provide the right incentives to keep users motivated long-term.

Impact

Although this project was created during a short design-a-thon sprint and did not go through development, it still had a meaningful impact on my growth as a designer. I practiced creating engaging financial literacy experiences, explored ways to gamify saving habits for millennials, and learned how to connect personal finance goals with collaborative community challenges. The process helped me strengthen my skills in UX research, rapid prototyping, and designing for user motivation. Most importantly, it inspired me to think about how design can make financial management feel approachable, empowering, and even fun.

Key Takeaways

Gamification can make overwhelming topics approachable

By turning financial tasks into progress-based challenges, we lowered emotional resistance.

Social motivation improves follow-through

Group islands encouraged accountability and made financial conversations less taboo.

Personalization increases relevance

Users were more likely to engage with financial literacy when it was tailored to their needs.
